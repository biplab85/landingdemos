NSW pool fence rules in plain English: what actually gets you a fail

Selling a property with a pool, leasing it out, or just renewing your compliance certificate? The pool fence inspection is the bit that catches most owners off guard. Not because the rules are unfair — but because the rules quietly changed in late 2023, and what passed in 2018 will not necessarily pass now.

This guide is what we wish every customer had read before we showed up to look at their fence. It is not legal advice — but it is what we see fail, every week.

The rules everyone knows

Most owners can recite the headline standards: 1.2m minimum height, no climbable gaps over 100mm, gates that self-close and self-latch. These remain accurate. The Act is AS 1926.1-2012 and it is enforced by your local council under the Swimming Pools Act 1992.

What people miss is the Non-Climbable Zone (NCZ) — and that is where most inspections fail.

The Non-Climbable Zone, decoded

Imagine a 900mm bubble around the outside of your pool fence — both up and out. Inside that bubble, nothing climbable is allowed. Nothing. Not a pot plant. Not the BBQ. Not a retaining wall that is more than 100mm tall. Not the edge of the spa pump housing.

The reason: a determined toddler will climb whatever you give them, and a 900mm bubble is roughly what a 2-year-old needs as launch space.

The three things that catch owners out most often:

1. Garden beds and retaining walls. If the wall on the outside of your pool fence is more than 100mm high, the effective height of the fence is measured from the top of the wall, not the ground. A 1.2m fence on a 200mm retaining wall is not 1.4m of barrier — it is 1.0m, and it will fail. 2. Outdoor furniture. A bench seat, an outdoor table, a kid’s sandpit — anything within 900mm of the outside of the fence that is taller than 100mm is a fail. We have seen pools fail because someone left a 20L pot plant within reach the week of the inspection. 3. Adjoining structures. If your pool fence joins a brick wall, that wall must also offer no climbable footholds within 1.2m of the top of the fence. Decorative brickwork and pebble-dash often trigger this.

What changed in 2023

Two small but important amendments:

  • Self-closing gates must now close from any starting position between 0 and 150mm — not just from fully open. We retrofit a tensioned hinge for $180–$240 per gate.
  • Latch height is still 1.5m from the ground on the pool side. But the measurement is now taken from the highest accessible point within 900mm of the latch, including the gate handle itself. This catches a lot of older drop-latches.

The two-minute self-check

Before booking a compliance inspection, do this:

  • Stand outside the gate. Push it. It should swing closed and click latched without you touching it again.
  • Walk the outside of the fence with a tape. Anywhere within 900mm horizontally and 1.2m vertically, find anything you could put a foot on. Move it.
  • Stand on the outside and try to reach the latch. If you can, a small kid can.

Once you can pass your own self-check, an inspection from us costs $295 and takes about 40 minutes. If we find something, we will quote it on the spot and most issues are sorted within a week.
