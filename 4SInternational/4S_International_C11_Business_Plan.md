## 1.0 Executive Summary

### 1.1 Industry Summary

Canada's anti-friction bearing market — encompassing ball bearings, roller bearings, and related components classified under Harmonized System code 8482 — represents a substantial segment of the country's industrial supply chain. In 2023, Canada imported US $803 million worth of HS 8482 products.^i^ Separately, IBISWorld estimates that domestic bearing manufacturing generated approximately $581.1 million in revenue across 17 establishments in 2025.^i^ The broader Canadian bearing market, including distribution and aftermarket services, was valued at approximately USD $3.88 billion in 2023, with projected growth at a compound annual growth rate of 4.5% through 2031.^ii^

Canada is structurally dependent on imported bearings, with an estimated 85–90% of domestic consumption fulfilled through imports.^iii^ The United States, China, and Japan together account for approximately 64% of Canada's total bearing imports. Domestic manufacturing has been contracting — the number of Canadian bearing manufacturers declined at a compound annual rate of -4.1% between 2020 and 2025, while manufacturing revenue fell at -1.5% over the same period.^iv^ This structural import dependency, combined with a shrinking domestic manufacturing base, creates a sustained and growing need for reliable bearing importers and distributors capable of serving Canadian industrial end-users.

The bearings consumed in Canada serve a diverse range of industries, including automotive manufacturing in Ontario, mining and heavy equipment operations in Western Canada and Northern Ontario, oil and gas extraction in Alberta, aerospace manufacturing in Quebec and Ontario, renewable energy installations, and agricultural machinery across the Prairies. Ball bearings represent the largest segment by revenue, while roller bearings — particularly spherical and tapered roller types — are experiencing faster growth due to demand from mining, construction, and heavy machinery applications.^v^

#### 1.1.1 Industry Problem

Despite Canada's significant bearing consumption, the domestic supply chain suffers from three structural deficiencies that inflate costs and constrain availability for small and medium-sized enterprises. Canadian end-user prices for bearings are estimated to be 15–40% higher than comparable US equivalents, driven primarily by the multi-tier, US-intermediated distribution structure that adds compounding markups at each stage.^vi^ Compounding this cost problem, an estimated 2.5% of the global bearing market consists of counterfeit products (SKF Group estimate), with the International Chamber of Commerce estimating $1.7 billion in annual economic and social costs from counterfeit bearings — underscoring the need for trusted, quality-verified distribution channels.^vi-a^

First, the majority of bearings entering Canada flow through a multi-tier supply chain — typically Asian Manufacturer → US Distributor → Canadian Branch → End-User — controlled by large multinational corporations including Applied Industrial Technologies (NYSE: AIT), Genuine Parts Company (NYSE: GPC), The Timken Company (NYSE: TKR), and SKF Group (Sweden). This multi-tier structure persists for several interconnected reasons. Major bearing manufacturers distribute exclusively through authorized dealer networks organized on a continental basis with US-headquartered management; under CUSMA, bearings moving from the United States to Canada enter duty-free for qualifying goods, which historically eliminated any tariff penalty for routing through the US hub; Asian manufacturers typically require full-container minimum order quantities of $30,000–$100,000 per shipment, volumes that are impractical for individual Canadian SME end-users purchasing for their own consumption but manageable for dedicated importers and large US-based aggregators who consolidate demand across multiple customers; and sourcing directly from Asian factories requires language capabilities, factory audit expertise, quality verification know-how, and supplier relationships that take years to develop — capabilities that most Canadian distributors have never needed to build because the US-intermediated channel provided a convenient alternative. The result is a supply chain optimized for large enterprise accounts purchasing premium-branded products, while small manufacturers, machine shops, food processors, and maintenance operations in the Greater Toronto Area and across Ontario frequently lack the purchasing volume to negotiate competitive pricing through these channels.

Second, the 2025–2026 United States trade war has introduced significant cost uncertainty into the traditional US-intermediated supply chain. The Canadian Federation of Independent Business reports that 63% of Canadian businesses experienced higher expenses due to tariffs, while 42% reported supply chain disruptions.^vii^ Many Canadian distributors historically sourced bearings — including Chinese-origin products — through American intermediaries, where embedded US Section 301 tariffs of 7.5% to 25% on HS 8482 bearings, and up to 100% on other Chinese product categories, inflated landed costs before re-export to Canada.^viii^ This intermediation markup persists even after Canada removed its general retaliatory surtax on most US goods on September 1, 2025, because the underlying US-side tariffs on Asian-origin bearings remain in effect.

Third, the Canadian bearing distribution market is notably underdeveloped in e-commerce and direct-import capabilities. Among established Ontario-based bearing distributors, only Bearings Canada Inc. of Woodbridge operates a meaningful online sales platform. The dominant distributors continue to rely on branch-based, sales-representative-driven models that are poorly suited to serving geographically dispersed SME customers requiring smaller, more frequent orders with rapid fulfillment.

### 1.2 Company Introduction

4S International Inc. is a federally incorporated Canadian corporation established to import and distribute cost-competitive anti-friction bearings directly from manufacturers in China, Japan, India, Thailand, and Vietnam to industrial end-users across Ontario and Canada. The Company will operate from Unit-11, 3400 Ridgeway Drive, Mississauga, Ontario — a location within the Pearson Airport industrial corridor that provides direct access to Highway 401, Highway 403, and Toronto Pearson International Airport, positioning the Company at the logistical centre of Canada's largest manufacturing and distribution region.

4S International will source anti-friction bearings — including deep groove ball bearings, angular contact ball bearings, tapered roller bearings, cylindrical roller bearings, spherical roller bearings, and bearing accessories — directly from qualified Asian manufacturers. By eliminating US-based intermediaries and importing directly into Canada under Most Favoured Nation duty rates of free to 6.5% depending on bearing type (or duty-free under CPTPP for Japanese and Vietnamese origins), the Company will achieve landed costs 15–30% below those available through the traditional multi-tier distribution channel. These cost savings will be passed to Canadian industrial customers, with the Company retaining a competitive gross margin of 48% in Year 1, improving to 50% by Year 3 onward as procurement volumes increase and supplier terms strengthen.

The direct-import model that 4S International intends to operate is not exclusive to this Company. Any entrepreneur or firm with Asian manufacturing sourcing experience — including Chinese bearing companies, trading houses, or other importers — could in principle establish a similar operation in Canada, and some may possess deeper supplier relationships or greater industry experience than Ms. Islam. Bearings Canada Inc. has operated a partial version of this model (as an authorized dealer for three Japanese brands) since 2012. The reason the large incumbent Canadian distributors — BDI/Canadian Bearings, Motion Canada, Wajax — have not pursued direct Asian sourcing is structural: their authorized dealer agreements with OEM manufacturers contractually bind them to purchasing through established channels, and importing generic Asian-origin bearings would risk their premium-brand distribution rights, which constitute the majority of their revenue.

Ms. Fatema Islam's qualification to execute this business model in Canada is supported by documented, verifiable evidence. Ms. Islam has operated S.S Bearing and Machineries — a bearing trading enterprise on Nawabpur Road in Dhaka, Bangladesh — since 2019, sourcing bearings directly from Chinese, Japanese, Thai, and Indian factories. She has spent over six years negotiating FOB pricing with Asian manufacturers, verifying quality against ABEC and ISO 492 standards, coordinating international shipping logistics, and managing customs documentation. Her existing business holds bearing inventory valued at approximately CAD $185,000 (through Ss Corporation), and her operations are registered with Bangladesh's National Board of Revenue, Dhaka South City Corporation, and VAT authorities. #### 1.2.2 Solution Statement

4S International addresses the three structural deficiencies identified in the Canadian bearing supply chain through a focused business model built on direct sourcing, SME-oriented service, and digital accessibility. The opportunity to import bearings directly from Asian manufacturers into Canada — rather than routing them through US intermediaries — is not unique to 4S International. Any importer with the requisite sourcing capabilities, logistics knowledge, and customs expertise can pursue this model. The opportunity exists as a direct consequence of the 2018–2026 US trade policy shift: prior to the imposition of US Section 301 tariffs on Chinese goods, there was no meaningful cost penalty for routing Asian-origin bearings through US distributors into Canada under CUSMA duty-free treatment. The Section 301 tariffs — ranging from 7.5% to 25% on HS 8482 bearings depending on subheading and tariff round — fundamentally altered this calculus by embedding a tariff cost into US-intermediated channels that does not apply when the same bearings are imported directly into Canada. 4S International will import bearings directly from qualified manufacturers in China, Japan, India, Thailand, and Vietnam, utilizing this direct-import approach. Under Canadian customs regulations, HS 8482 bearings are not listed in the Steel Derivative Goods Surtax Order (SOR/2025-267), and bearings entering Canada from non-US sources attract Most Favoured Nation duties of free to 6.5% depending on bearing type — ball bearings (HS 8482.10), spherical roller bearings (HS 8482.30), and bearing parts (HS 8482.91/99) enter duty-free under MFN, while tapered roller bearings (HS 8482.20) and cylindrical roller bearings (HS 8482.50) attract 6.5% MFN duty — with all bearing types entering duty-free from Japan and Vietnam under CPTPP, and at free or near-free rates from EU origins under CETA (Germany, Italy, Sweden).^ix^

The Company will target the underserved small and medium-sized enterprise segment — machine shops, food processors, packaging operations, agricultural equipment dealers, and general maintenance contractors — that lack the purchasing volume to access competitive pricing from the large multinational distributors. 4S International will offer minimum order quantities as low as a single case (typically 10–50 units depending on bearing type), with same-day or next-day delivery within the Greater Toronto Area and two-to-three-day delivery across Ontario via established courier partnerships.

To address the e-commerce gap in the Canadian bearing market, 4S International will operate a transactional website with real-time inventory visibility, technical specifications, cross-reference search functionality, and online ordering capabilities. This platform will complement the Company's direct B2B sales force and trade show presence, enabling the Company to serve customers across Canada without the overhead of a multi-branch distribution network.

### 3.4 Value Proposition

4S International's value proposition is built on four differentiated elements that address specific deficiencies in the current Canadian bearing supply chain.

**Direct-Source Cost Advantage.** By sourcing bearings directly from Asian manufacturers and importing into Canada under MFN duty rates of free to 6.5% depending on bearing type — or duty-free under CPTPP (Japan, Vietnam) and CETA (EU) trade agreements — 4S International achieves landed costs 15–30% below those available through the traditional multi-tier supply chain (Asian Manufacturer → US Distributor → Canadian Branch → End-User) that embeds US Section 301 tariff markups of 7.5–25% on Chinese-origin goods. ## 4.0 Industry Overview

### 4.1 Industry Summary

The Canadian anti-friction bearing industry encompasses the manufacturing, importation, and distribution of ball bearings, roller bearings, and related mechanical components used across virtually every sector of the Canadian economy that involves rotating machinery. The industry operates under NAICS code 332991 (Ball Bearing Manufacturing) for domestic production and NAICS 417230 (Industrial Machinery, Equipment, and Supplies Merchant Wholesalers) for distribution and importation activities.

According to IBISWorld, the domestic Ball Bearing Manufacturing industry in Canada generated approximately $581.1 million in revenue in 2025, produced by 17 establishments — a figure that has declined at a compound annual growth rate of -4.1% over the 2020–2025 period.^xiii^ The broader Canadian bearing market, encompassing all distribution channels, aftermarket services, and imported products, was estimated at USD $3.88 billion in 2023 by Grand View Research, with a projected compound annual growth rate of 7.4% through 2030.^xiv^ A more conservative estimate from 6Wresearch projects growth at 4.5% CAGR for the 2025–2031 period.^xv^

Canada imported US $803 million of HS 8482 products (ball or roller bearings) in 2023, representing a 4.2% decline from the US $838 million imported in 2022.^xvi^ Canada simultaneously exported US $353 million of bearings, with the United States receiving 78% of those exports.^xvii^ The differential between imports and exports — a net import position of approximately US $450 million — underscores Canada's structural dependence on foreign-manufactured bearings to meet domestic demand.

### 4.2 Industry Performance

#### 4.2.1 Revenue & Growth Trends

Canadian bearing market revenue has exhibited a divergent pattern between domestic manufacturing and the broader distribution market. Domestic manufacturing revenue declined at -1.5% CAGR between 2020 and 2025, driven by the inability of Canadian plants to compete with lower-cost imports and the rationalization of production facilities by multinational owners.^xviii^ However, IBISWorld projects that the domestic manufacturing segment is expected to grow over the next five years (2025–2030), driven by nearshoring trends, tariff protection effects, and increased demand from mining and renewable energy sectors.

The broader bearing market — including imports and distribution — has grown at a moderate pace, supported by post-pandemic industrial recovery, infrastructure investment, and automotive sector activity. Grand View Research's 7.4% CAGR projection reflects anticipated growth across all bearing types and channels, while 6Wresearch's more conservative 4.5% estimate focuses specifically on the Canadian distribution market.

Import volumes have shown resilience despite trade disruptions. Canada's HS 8482 imports declined only 4.2% from 2022 to 2023 despite global supply chain pressures, indicating stable underlying industrial demand. The top three source countries — the United States, China, and Japan — together account for approximately 64% of total bearing imports into Canada.^xix^

#### 4.2.2 Employment & Wage Trends

The Canadian bearing manufacturing sector employed workers across 17 establishments in 2025, with employment declining alongside the contraction in the number of firms.^xx^ The distribution and wholesale sector, by contrast, is a significantly larger employer. Major distributors such as Canadian Bearings / BDI Canada (approximately 298 employees across North America, 38 branches), NTN Bearing Corporation of Canada (approximately 107 employees), Motion Canada (50+ branches, 3 distribution centres, 9 repair centres), and Wajax Corporation (multiple Ontario branches) collectively employ thousands of workers in sales, warehousing, logistics, and technical support roles across Canada.^xxi^

Wages in the bearing distribution sector reflect the industrial wholesale labour market in the Greater Toronto Area. The Government of Canada Job Bank reports median hourly wages for relevant occupations in the Toronto CMA as follows: warehouse workers (NOC 75101) at $17.50–$20.00, sales representatives (NOC 62100) at $21.00–$25.00, and operations supervisors (NOC 72020) at $26.00–$30.00. 4S International's wage structure is set at or above these median benchmarks to ensure competitiveness in the local labour market.

#### 4.2.3 Profitability Benchmarks

Industrial wholesale distribution in Canada typically operates on gross margins of 25–40%, depending on the level of value-added services, product specialization, and customer mix. Bearing distributors handling premium OEM brands (SKF, NTN, Timken, Schaeffler) generally achieve gross margins at the lower end of this range (25–30%) due to the premium pricing of branded products and the competitive intensity among authorized distributors. Independent distributors handling a mix of branded and non-branded products can achieve margins of 35–42% by sourcing lower-cost alternatives and targeting underserved customer segments.^xxii^

Publicly traded incumbents provide verified benchmarks for industry profitability. Applied Industrial Technologies (NYSE: AIT) — North America's largest bearing distributor — reported a gross profit margin of 30.3%, a net profit margin of approximately 8.6%, and an EBITDA margin of approximately 12.3% for fiscal year 2025.^xxii-a^ Genuine Parts Company (NYSE: GPC), parent of Motion Industries, reported a gross margin of 36.3% for fiscal year 2024, with its industrial segment generating approximately $8.8 billion in annual revenue.^xxii-b^ Wajax Corporation (TSX: WJX), an independent Canadian distributor, reported industrial parts revenue of $404 million in fiscal year 2025, a 3.4% year-over-year decline, and was ranked No. 4 on MDM's 2025 Power Transmission/Bearings distributor list.^xxii-c^ The Timken Company (NYSE: TKR) reported EBITDA margins of 16.6–18.2% across its bearing and power transmission segments.^xxii-d^

This margin level is achievable because the Company's cost base consists of generic, functionally equivalent bearings purchased directly from ISO 9001 certified Asian factories at FOB pricing, rather than branded OEM products purchased through multi-tier authorized channels. Applied Industrial's 30.3% gross margin is earned on a cost base of branded products purchased at premium OEM pricing; 4S International's 48–50% gross margin is earned on a fundamentally lower cost base. At the top tier, four multinational manufacturers maintain direct Canadian operations: SKF Canada Limited (Scarborough, Ontario), NTN Bearing Corporation of Canada (Mississauga, Ontario), Timken Canada LP (Mississauga, Ontario), and Schaeffler Canada Inc. The distribution tier is dominated by three large, publicly traded industrial distributors: Applied Industrial Technologies (NYSE: AIT), which acquired BDI Canada / Canadian Bearings in 2023; Genuine Parts Company (NYSE: GPC), which operates Motion Canada with 50+ branches; and Wajax Corporation (TSX: WJX), an independent Canadian distributor with multiple Ontario locations. ### 4.3 Key Market Drivers & Trends

**Driver 1: Industrial Manufacturing Recovery and Expansion.** Canada's manufacturing sector has experienced renewed investment following the pandemic-era contraction, driven by nearshoring trends, government infrastructure spending, and the electrification of the automotive sector. Ontario's manufacturing output — particularly in automotive, food processing, and general fabrication — drives sustained demand for replacement bearings and new-equipment specifications. Every electric motor, conveyor system, pump, compressor, and rotating machine in these facilities requires bearings that must be periodically replaced, creating a recurring maintenance demand that is largely insensitive to economic cycles.

**Driver 2: Mining and Resource Sector Activity.** Canada's mining sector, concentrated in Northern Ontario, Quebec, and Western Canada, is a significant consumer of heavy-duty bearings — particularly spherical roller bearings, tapered roller bearings, and large-bore cylindrical roller bearings used in crushers, grinding mills, conveyors, and vibrating screens. The critical mineral extraction push (lithium, cobalt, nickel) supporting electric vehicle battery production is expected to sustain and increase mining-related bearing demand over the projection period.^xxv^

**Driver 3: Supply Chain Diversification Away from US Intermediaries.** The 2025–2026 US–Canada tariff confrontation accelerated Canadian industrial buyers' interest in diversifying supply chains away from US-dependent channels. The Canadian Federation of Independent Business reports that 42% of businesses experienced supply chain disruptions due to tariffs, while McKinsey's Supply Chain Risk Pulse survey found that 38% of companies plan to reduce their supply chain presence in China and 39% are pursuing dual-sourcing strategies.^xxvi^ These trends create opportunity for a Canadian-based importer that can offer direct Asian sourcing as an alternative to the US-intermediated channel.

**Driver 4: Aging Infrastructure and Maintenance Demand.** Much of Canada's industrial equipment base — including manufacturing machinery, HVAC systems, building infrastructure, and agricultural equipment — is aging and requires increasing levels of maintenance, including bearing replacement. The Canadian Infrastructure Report Card identifies significant maintenance backlogs across municipal, institutional, and industrial facilities, all of which contain rotating equipment that consumes bearings on a replacement cycle basis.

**Driver 5: Electric Vehicle and Renewable Energy Transition.** The transition to electric vehicles and renewable energy generation creates both shifting and growing demand for bearings. Electric vehicles use fewer but more specialized bearings than internal combustion vehicles, while wind turbines require large-diameter main shaft bearings, pitch bearings, and yaw bearings. Canada's investment in wind energy capacity and the concentration of EV battery and assembly facilities in Ontario are expected to generate incremental bearing demand over the projection period.^xxvii^

**Driver 6: E-Commerce Adoption in Industrial Procurement.** The shift toward online procurement in the industrial sector has moved beyond early adoption. Industry reports estimate that 72.4% of global bearing market sales in 2023 involved some form of digital channel — a figure that includes B2B purchasing portals, EDI (electronic data interchange) systems, and online catalogs, not solely consumer-style e-commerce platforms.^xxvii-a^ B2B industrial e-commerce revenue reached approximately $15 billion in 2024, projected to grow to $28 billion by 2033 at a 7.1% compound annual growth rate.^xxvii-a^ Industrial buyers increasingly expect the ability to search for products by specification, view real-time inventory and pricing, and place orders online without requiring a sales representative. This trend particularly benefits newer entrants like 4S International that can build digital-first distribution models without the legacy cost structures of branch-based networks.

**Driver 7: Counterfeit Product Risk Driving Demand for Trusted Distributors.** The global counterfeit bearing problem — estimated at 2.5% of the worldwide bearing market by SKF Group, with approximately $1.7 billion in annual economic and social costs according to the International Chamber of Commerce — is driving industrial buyers toward trusted, quality-verified distribution channels.^xxvii-b^ In December 2025, Chinese authorities seized major shipments of counterfeit bearings at Ningbo Port as part of ongoing enforcement efforts, highlighting that the problem persists even in manufacturing-origin countries.^xxvii-c^ For Canadian end-users purchasing unbranded or generic bearings, the ability to verify product authenticity and quality at the point of distribution is a critical value proposition. 4S International's model — sourcing directly from ISO 9001 certified factories with pre-shipment quality verification, rather than through opaque intermediary chains — addresses this concern directly.

**Driver 8: Manufacturer Investment Signals Canadian Market Growth.** Recent investments by major bearing manufacturers in their Canadian operations indicate growing confidence in the Canadian market. NTN Bearing Corporation of Canada re-opened its Edmonton Distribution Centre (12,500+ square feet) to better serve Western Canadian customers, while NSK is expanding its Canadian operations with a new bilingual website and enhanced technical support capabilities.^xxvii-d^ These investments by established manufacturers validate the growth trajectory of the Canadian bearing market and indicate that manufacturers see sufficient demand to justify expanded local infrastructure — conditions that equally support a new entrant focused on the underserved SME distribution segment.

### 4.5 Why Canada & Regional Fit

Canada's structural import dependency for bearings — with 85–90% of consumption met through imports — creates a sustained market for importation and distribution enterprises. Unlike domestic manufacturing, which requires substantial capital investment in precision machining equipment and faces competitive pressure from lower-cost producers, bearing importation and distribution is a capital-efficient business model that leverages Canada's open trade framework and well-developed logistics infrastructure.

Mississauga, Ontario, is the optimal location for a bearing import and distribution operation in Canada for several reasons. The city sits at the intersection of Highway 401 (Canada's busiest freight corridor) and Highway 403, providing road access to the GTA manufacturing base, the Hamilton–Niagara industrial region, and the Kitchener–Waterloo technology and manufacturing corridor. Toronto Pearson International Airport, adjacent to the Company's planned location, is Canada's largest cargo airport and handles the majority of air freight entering the country. The Port of Montreal and Port of Hamilton — Canada's primary container ports for Asian-origin cargo — are within one-day trucking distance of Mississauga.

The Ridgeway Drive location is situated within one of the highest concentrations of warehousing and distribution operations in Canada, which provides access to established freight carrier services, customs brokerage firms, and industrial service providers. The location also positions the Company within the Peel Region, which has one of the most diverse and available labour pools in Canada for warehouse, logistics, and sales positions.

From a trade policy perspective, Canada's portfolio of free trade agreements provides a structural advantage for a direct-import model. Bearings from Japan enter Canada under the Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP) at free or significantly reduced duty rates. Bearings from the European Union (Germany, Italy, Sweden) enter under the Canada–European Union Comprehensive Economic and Trade Agreement (CETA) at free or near-free rates. Bearings from most other countries, including China, India, and Thailand, enter under Most Favoured Nation rates of free to 6.5% depending on bearing type — still significantly below the layered tariff burden faced by the same goods transiting through the United States.^xxxi^

However, Bearings Canada's authorized dealer model constrains its pricing flexibility — the company must purchase through its three authorized brands' pricing structures rather than negotiating FOB pricing directly with factories. Its supplier range is limited to three brands from Japanese manufacturers, compared to 4S International's planned direct sourcing from manufacturers across five countries. This structural difference in sourcing gives 4S International a cost advantage that Bearings Canada cannot replicate without fundamentally changing its business model.

**Chinese Manufacturer Presence in North America.** Major Chinese bearing manufacturers maintain US-based offices — C&U Americas LLC (Plymouth, MI), ZWZ Bearing USA (City of Industry, CA), and LYC North America Inc. (Geneva, IL) — but none currently operate Canadian distribution facilities.^xxxvii-a^ These manufacturers' core competency is B2B production at scale (C&U Group alone produces 1.6 billion bearings annually), and they sell into the Canadian market through existing US-based distribution channels. However, there is no structural barrier preventing a Chinese manufacturer or Chinese-backed trading company from establishing direct Canadian distribution operations. The fact that none have done so to date likely reflects that (a) the Canadian market is small relative to their global output, (b) last-mile SME distribution requires local infrastructure — warehousing, customs compliance, bilingual customer service — that differs from their manufacturing expertise, and (c) the existing US-intermediated channel already moves their products into Canada, albeit at higher cost to end-users. If 4S International or a similar entrant demonstrates that direct Canadian distribution of Asian-origin bearings is profitable, larger and better-capitalized competitors — including Chinese manufacturers themselves — could enter the market. The business plan accounts for this competitive risk: the Company's revenue projections require only 0.187% market share by Year 5, a level that does not depend on excluding competitors from the market.

### 5.3 Competitive Advantage

4S International's competitive position is differentiated from the established market participants on four dimensions.

First, the Company's direct sourcing model — purchasing generic, functionally equivalent bearings at FOB factory pricing rather than branded OEM pricing — produces a structural cost advantage of 15–30% relative to incumbents. The primary driver of this advantage is brand premium bypass: 4S International purchases the same specification bearings (e.g., 6205-2RS, 25×52×15mm) from ISO 9001 certified factories at factory-direct pricing, while incumbents like BDI/Applied Industrial and Motion Canada purchase through authorized OEM channels at premium branded pricing. As a secondary benefit, importing directly into Canada under MFN duties of free to 6.5% avoids the embedded US Section 301 tariffs of 7.5–25% that inflate costs for Chinese-origin bearings transiting through American intermediaries.

The large incumbent Canadian distributors — BDI/Canadian Bearings, Motion Canada, Wajax — have not replicated this direct-import model because their operations are structured as branch extensions of US parent companies with continental supply chains, and their authorized distributor agreements with OEM manufacturers (SKF, NTN, Timken) contractually bind them to purchasing through established channels. Importing generic Asian-origin bearings alongside premium branded products would risk their authorized dealer status — the single strongest structural barrier, rooted in incentive incompatibility rather than capability.

The direct-import opportunity is, however, open to any independent operator with Asian sourcing capability — including Chinese manufacturers or trading companies that may possess deeper supplier relationships and greater scale than 4S International. The reason this gap has persisted is not that no one possesses the sourcing expertise, but that (a) large incumbents face structural barriers to adopting the model, (b) Chinese manufacturers have focused on B2B production rather than Canadian last-mile distribution (as discussed in Section 5.2), and (c) the Canadian SME bearing segment, while underserved, is not large enough to attract major new entrants who would need to build local warehousing, logistics, customs compliance, and customer service infrastructure from scratch. 4S International's business case does not depend on being the only company capable of direct Asian sourcing; it depends on executing a viable business model in a market where the opportunity currently exists and where the Company's founder has documented, operational sourcing capability.

Second, the Company's multi-country sourcing portfolio provides supply chain redundancy that is unavailable to distributors authorized for a single manufacturer's product line. If trade conditions, quality issues, or production disruptions affect any single source country, the Company can shift procurement to alternative suppliers without interrupting customer service.

Third, the Company's digital-first distribution model — built around a transactional e-commerce platform with real-time inventory visibility and cross-reference search — addresses the documented e-commerce gap in the Canadian bearing market. Among the major distributors, online purchasing is typically limited to gated portals requiring pre-established accounts; among SME distributors, only Bearings Canada operates a meaningful online store.

Fourth, the Company's purpose-built focus on the SME customer segment — with minimum order quantities as low as a single case, dedicated account management, and flexible payment terms — addresses the service gap created by the consolidation of the distribution tier around enterprise-focused, multi-branch operators.

### 6.2 Go-to-Market Breakdown

The Company's go-to-market strategy is organized into three phases, progressing from operational foundations through market expansion to sustained growth and management transition.

#### 6.2.1 Short-Term Strategy — Year 1

The Company's Year 1 strategy focuses on establishing operational foundations and generating initial revenue through direct B2B sales to small and medium-sized industrial customers in the Greater Toronto Area. The priority during this period is to prove the business model by demonstrating that direct Asian sourcing delivers a meaningful cost advantage that translates into customer acquisition.

During the first six months, the Company will complete all incorporation, facility setup, and initial inventory procurement activities. The initial inventory investment of $80,000 will be allocated across the highest-demand bearing types — primarily deep groove ball bearings (6200, 6300, and 6000 series), tapered roller bearings (30200 and 32000 series), and pillow block bearing units. Year 1 sourcing will realistically begin with 1–2 primary Chinese suppliers, using a mix of full-container-load (FCL) and less-than-container-load (LCL) shipments through freight consolidators to manage initial order volumes. Multi-country diversification — adding suppliers in Japan, Vietnam, India, and Thailand — will be phased in from Year 2 onward as order volumes justify full-container orders from additional sources. This inventory selection targets the most common replacement bearing sizes used in general manufacturing, food processing, and agricultural equipment applications.

Revenue generation will begin in Month 1 with early orders from initial contacts, ramping from $5,000 per month to $55,000 by Month 12 as the Company's customer base grows through direct outreach by Ms. Islam to industrial facilities, machine shops, and maintenance contractors in the Mississauga–Brampton–Etobicoke industrial corridor. The Company's introductory pricing — set at 15–20% below prevailing distributor prices for equivalent products — will serve as the primary customer acquisition tool. The Year 1 revenue target of $300,000 requires approximately 10–15 active customer accounts by year-end with average monthly purchases of $2,500–$3,500 each.

#### 6.2.2 Medium-Term Strategy — Years 2-3

The medium-term strategy focuses on scaling revenue, expanding the product range, and building the organizational capacity required for sustained growth. By the end of Year 2, the Company targets $600,000 in annual revenue with 2.50 FTE employees and an active customer base of 35–45 accounts.

Key initiatives during Years 2–3 include the expansion of the supplier portfolio to include manufacturers in Vietnam, Thailand, and India, providing broader product selection and additional tariff advantages under CPTPP. The sales team will be expanded with a dedicated Sales Representative to support account growth while Ms. Islam focuses on supplier management, strategic accounts, and operational oversight. The Company will invest in digital marketing — including Google Ads targeting bearing-related search terms and an SEO strategy focused on long-tail product queries — to drive traffic to the e-commerce platform and reduce customer acquisition cost.

During Year 3, the Company will hire a Sales Representative and a part-time Bookkeeper/Admin to support the growing transactional volume. Revenue is projected to reach $900,000 by Year 3, with gross margins reaching 50% as increased procurement volumes enable consistent FCL shipping and volume rebates. The Company projects net income of $21,238 in Year 3.

#### 6.2.3 Long-Term Strategy — Years 4-5

The long-term strategy focuses on consolidating the Company's market position, achieving sustained profitability, and completing the management transition required under the C-11 work permit exit plan. By Year 5, the Company targets $1,500,000 in annual revenue with 6.75 FTE employees, gross margins of 50%, and net income of $113,601.

Key initiatives during Years 4–5 include geographic expansion beyond the GTA to serve industrial customers across Ontario and into Quebec and Western Canada through e-commerce and courier delivery. The team will be expanded with a Shipping/Receiving Clerk and a part-time Quality Inspector to support a growing customer base projected to reach 60–75 active accounts. The Company will also pursue strategic wholesale relationships with smaller regional distributors who can serve as resellers of 4S International's products in geographic markets that do not justify direct coverage.

By Year 4, the Company's experienced staff — including the Sales & Marketing Coordinator, Sales Representative, and Bookkeeper/Admin — will have developed sufficient operational capability to support the management transition contemplated in the exit plan. Ms. Islam will begin transitioning day-to-day operational responsibilities to a Canadian-hired Operations Manager recruited from the local labour market, enabling her gradual shift to a passive shareholder role. The Company's mature-state operations will be characterized by established supplier relationships, proven logistics processes, a loyal customer base, and a team capable of operating independently.

#### 9.3.2 Revenue Justification

The revenue projections are supported by three benchmarking analyses that demonstrate their conservatism and achievability.

**Revenue per Employee.** 4S International's projected revenue per full-time equivalent employee ranges from $171,429 in Year 1 to $222,222 in Year 5. This falls within the $200,000–$400,000 industry benchmark for small industrial distributors, with the Year 1 figure slightly below the range due to the startup ramp period during which the Company carries its full initial staffing complement but generates revenue for only eight months. By Year 2 and beyond, revenue per employee stabilizes within the benchmark range, confirming that the Company's projections are conservative and do not assume productivity levels that exceed industry norms.

**Market Share.** Canada imported approximately US $803 million of HS 8482 bearings in 2023.^xli^ 4S International's Year 5 revenue target of $1,500,000 represents approximately 0.187% of this total import market. However, the $803 million figure encompasses the entire Canadian bearing import market — including premium OEM bearings for automotive assembly lines, aerospace-grade precision bearings, and large-volume enterprise procurement contracts served by authorized distributors of SKF, Timken, NTN, and Schaeffler. 4S International is not competing for this business. The Company's actual competitive arena is the much narrower segment of non-branded and generic bearing sales to small and medium-sized enterprises — machine shops, food processors, maintenance contractors, and small manufacturers purchasing standard MRO bearings where specification compliance matters more than brand name.

Estimating this addressable market requires segmenting the total market by customer size and product type. As described in Section 4.4.3, the Canadian bearing market divides into three customer tiers: large enterprise (annual bearing spend exceeding $500,000), mid-market ($50,000–$500,000), and small/micro (below $50,000). The small/micro segment — 4S International's primary target — is the most fragmented and least well-served by existing distributors. A conservative estimate of the addressable market can be derived as follows:

| Segmentation Step | Estimate | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Total Canadian HS 8482 import market | $803,000,000 | TrendEconomy, 2023 |
| Less: OEM/automotive/aerospace (branded, spec-controlled) | ~55% | Industry reports; automotive (20-25% of market), aerospace, OEM lines require certified branded products |
| Less: Large enterprise accounts (>$500K spend, served by national distributors) | ~40% of remainder | Served by BDI, Motion, Wajax through dedicated account teams with negotiated pricing |
| Less: Mid-market accounts ($50K–$500K, partially served) | ~50% of remainder | Price sensitivity exists but established distributor relationships and authorized brands preferred |
| Less: Buyers requiring specific branded products | ~25% of remainder | Even among small buyers, some applications require OEM-specified brands |
| Remaining: Addressable SME segment for non-branded/generic bearings | ~$81–$109 million | Small/micro buyers, price-sensitive, specification-driven rather than brand-driven |

The range reflects uncertainty in the segmentation percentages — the lower bound assumes a more conservative share available to non-branded distributors, while the upper bound assumes higher price sensitivity among small buyers. Within this estimated $80–$110 million addressable segment, 4S International's Year 5 target of $1,500,000 represents approximately 1.4–1.9% — a small but meaningful share that requires acquiring 60–75 active customer accounts from the thousands of industrial facilities in the GTA and Ontario corridor.

The competitors in this specific segment are not the large incumbents (BDI, Motion, Wajax) selling branded products to enterprise accounts. They are: (a) **Bearings Canada Inc.** — the closest existing competitor, operating as an authorized dealer for three Japanese brands since 2012; (b) **online marketplaces** such as Amazon.ca and eBay.ca where generic bearings are sold without technical support or B2B terms; (c) **direct Alibaba/AliExpress purchases** by Canadian buyers willing to handle their own customs clearance and accept 2–6 week delivery times; and (d) **any future entrant** — including Chinese manufacturers, other Asian-diaspora trading companies, or any entrepreneur with sourcing capabilities — who pursues the same direct-import model. The business model is replicable, and 4S International's projections do not assume the absence of competition in this segment.
