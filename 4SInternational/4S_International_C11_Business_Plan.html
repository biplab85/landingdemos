1.0 Executive Summary

1.1 Industry Summary

Canada's anti-friction bearing market - encompassing ball bearings, roller bearings, and related components classified under Harmonized System code 8482 - represents a substantial segment of the country's industrial supply chain. In 2023, Canada imported US $803 million worth of HS 8482 products.i Separately, IBISWorld estimates that domestic bearing manufacturing generated approximately $581.1 million in revenue across 17 establishments in 2025.i The broader Canadian bearing market, including distribution and aftermarket services, was valued at approximately USD $3.88 billion in 2023, with projected growth at a compound annual growth rate of 4.5% through 2031.ii

Canada is structurally dependent on imported bearings, with an estimated 85-90% of domestic consumption fulfilled through imports.iii The United States, China, and Japan together account for approximately 64% of Canada's total bearing imports. Domestic manufacturing has been contracting - the number of Canadian bearing manufacturers declined at a compound annual rate of -4.1% between 2020 and 2025, while manufacturing revenue fell at -1.5% over the same period.iv This structural import dependency, combined with a shrinking domestic manufacturing base, creates a sustained and growing need for reliable bearing importers and distributors capable of serving Canadian industrial end-users.

The bearings consumed in Canada serve a diverse range of industries, including automotive manufacturing in Ontario, mining and heavy equipment operations in Western Canada and Northern Ontario, oil and gas extraction in Alberta, aerospace manufacturing in Quebec and Ontario, renewable energy installations, and agricultural machinery across the Prairies. Ball bearings represent the largest segment by revenue, while roller bearings - particularly spherical and tapered roller types - are experiencing faster growth due to demand from mining, construction, and heavy machinery applications.v

1.1.1 Industry Problem

Despite Canada's significant bearing consumption, the domestic supply chain suffers from three structural deficiencies that inflate costs and constrain availability for small and medium-sized enterprises. Canadian end-user prices for bearings are estimated to be 15-40% higher than comparable US equivalents, driven primarily by the multi-tier, US-intermediated distribution structure that adds compounding markups at each stage.vi Compounding this cost problem, an estimated 2.5% of the global bearing market consists of counterfeit products (SKF Group estimate), with the International Chamber of Commerce estimating $1.7 billion in annual economic and social costs from counterfeit bearings - underscoring the need for trusted, quality-verified distribution channels.vi-a

First, the majority of bearings entering Canada flow through a multi-tier supply chain - typically Asian Manufacturer → US Distributor → Canadian Branch → End-User - controlled by large multinational corporations including Applied Industrial Technologies (NYSE: AIT), Genuine Parts Company (NYSE: GPC), The Timken Company (NYSE: TKR), and SKF Group (Sweden). This multi-tier structure persists for several interconnected reasons. Major bearing manufacturers distribute exclusively through authorized dealer networks organized on a continental basis with US-headquartered management; under CUSMA, bearings moving from the United States to Canada enter duty-free for qualifying goods, which historically eliminated any tariff penalty for routing through the US hub; Asian manufacturers typically require full-container minimum order quantities of $30,000-$100,000 per shipment, volumes that are impractical for individual Canadian SME end-users purchasing for their own consumption but manageable for dedicated importers and large US-based aggregators who consolidate demand across multiple customers; and sourcing directly from Asian factories requires language capabilities, factory audit expertise, quality verification know-how, and supplier relationships that take years to develop - capabilities that most Canadian distributors have never needed to build because the US-intermediated channel provided a convenient alternative. The result is a supply chain optimized for large enterprise accounts purchasing premium-branded products, while small manufacturers, machine shops, food processors, and maintenance operations in the Greater Toronto Area and across Ontario frequently lack the purchasing volume to negotiate competitive pricing through these channels.

Second, the 2025-2026 United States trade war has introduced significant cost uncertainty into the traditional US-intermediated supply chain. The Canadian Federation of Independent Business reports that 63% of Canadian businesses experienced higher expenses due to tariffs, while 42% reported supply chain disruptions.vii Many Canadian distributors historically sourced bearings - including Chinese-origin products - through American intermediaries, where embedded US Section 301 tariffs of 7.5% to 25% on HS 8482 bearings, and up to 100% on other Chinese product categories, inflated landed costs before re-export to Canada.viii This intermediation markup persists even after Canada removed its general retaliatory surtax on most US goods on September 1, 2025, because the underlying US-side tariffs on Asian-origin bearings remain in effect.

Third, the Canadian bearing distribution market is notably underdeveloped in e-commerce and direct-import capabilities. Among established Ontario-based bearing distributors, only Bearings Canada Inc. of Woodbridge operates a meaningful online sales platform. The dominant distributors continue to rely on branch-based, sales-representative-driven models that are poorly suited to serving geographically dispersed SME customers requiring smaller, more frequent orders with rapid fulfillment.

By eliminating US-based intermediaries and importing directly into Canada under Most Favoured Nation duty rates of free to 6.5% depending on bearing type (or duty-free under CPTPP for Japanese and Vietnamese origins), the Company will achieve landed costs 15-30% below those available through the traditional multi-tier distribution channel. The direct-import model that 4S International intends to operate is not exclusive to this Company. Any entrepreneur or firm with Asian manufacturing sourcing experience - including Chinese bearing companies, trading houses, or other importers - could in principle establish a similar operation in Canada, and some may possess deeper supplier relationships or greater industry experience than Ms. Islam. Bearings Canada Inc. has operated a partial version of this model (as an authorized dealer for three Japanese brands) since 2012. The reason the large incumbent Canadian distributors - BDI/Canadian Bearings, Motion Canada, Wajax - have not pursued direct Asian sourcing is structural: their authorized dealer agreements with OEM manufacturers contractually bind them to purchasing through established channels, and importing generic Asian-origin bearings would risk their premium-brand distribution rights, which constitute the majority of their revenue.

1.2.2 Solution Statement

4S International addresses the three structural deficiencies identified in the Canadian bearing supply chain through a focused business model built on direct sourcing, SME-oriented service, and digital accessibility. The opportunity to import bearings directly from Asian manufacturers into Canada - rather than routing them through US intermediaries - is not unique to 4S International. Any importer with the requisite sourcing capabilities, logistics knowledge, and customs expertise can pursue this model. The opportunity exists as a direct consequence of the 2018-2026 US trade policy shift: prior to the imposition of US Section 301 tariffs on Chinese goods, there was no meaningful cost penalty for routing Asian-origin bearings through US distributors into Canada under CUSMA duty-free treatment. The Section 301 tariffs - ranging from 7.5% to 25% on HS 8482 bearings depending on subheading and tariff round - fundamentally altered this calculus by embedding a tariff cost into US-intermediated channels that does not apply when the same bearings are imported directly into Canada. 4S International will import bearings directly from qualified manufacturers in China, Japan, India, Thailand, and Vietnam, utilizing this direct-import approach. Under Canadian customs regulations, HS 8482 bearings are not listed in the Steel Derivative Goods Surtax Order (SOR/2025-267), and bearings entering Canada from non-US sources attract Most Favoured Nation duties of free to 6.5% depending on bearing type - ball bearings (HS 8482.10), spherical roller bearings (HS 8482.30), and bearing parts (HS 8482.91/99) enter duty-free under MFN, while tapered roller bearings (HS 8482.20) and cylindrical roller bearings (HS 8482.50) attract 6.5% MFN duty - with all bearing types entering duty-free from Japan and Vietnam under CPTPP, and at free or near-free rates from EU origins under CETA (Germany, Italy, Sweden).ix

The Company will target the underserved small and medium-sized enterprise segment - machine shops, food processors, packaging operations, agricultural equipment dealers, and general maintenance contractors - that lack the purchasing volume to access competitive pricing from the large multinational distributors. 4S International will offer minimum order quantities as low as a single case (typically 10-50 units depending on bearing type), with same-day or next-day delivery within the Greater Toronto Area and two-to-three-day delivery across Ontario via established courier partnerships.

To address the e-commerce gap in the Canadian bearing market, 4S International will operate a transactional website with real-time inventory visibility, technical specifications, cross-reference search functionality, and online ordering capabilities. This platform will complement the Company's direct B2B sales force and trade show presence, enabling the Company to serve customers across Canada without the overhead of a multi-branch distribution network.

4.0 Industry Overview

4.1 Industry Summary

The Canadian anti-friction bearing industry encompasses the manufacturing, importation, and distribution of ball bearings, roller bearings, and related mechanical components used across virtually every sector of the Canadian economy that involves rotating machinery. The industry operates under NAICS code 332991 (Ball Bearing Manufacturing) for domestic production and NAICS 417230 (Industrial Machinery, Equipment, and Supplies Merchant Wholesalers) for distribution and importation activities.

According to IBISWorld, the domestic Ball Bearing Manufacturing industry in Canada generated approximately $581.1 million in revenue in 2025, produced by 17 establishments - a figure that has declined at a compound annual growth rate of -4.1% over the 2020-2025 period.xiii The broader Canadian bearing market, encompassing all distribution channels, aftermarket services, and imported products, was estimated at USD $3.88 billion in 2023 by Grand View Research, with a projected compound annual growth rate of 7.4% through 2030.xiv A more conservative estimate from 6Wresearch projects growth at 4.5% CAGR for the 2025-2031 period.xv

Canada imported US $803 million of HS 8482 products (ball or roller bearings) in 2023, representing a 4.2% decline from the US $838 million imported in 2022.xvi Canada simultaneously exported US $353 million of bearings, with the United States receiving 78% of those exports.xvii The differential between imports and exports - a net import position of approximately US $450 million - underscores Canada's structural dependence on foreign-manufactured bearings to meet domestic demand.

Canadian bearing market revenue has exhibited a divergent pattern between domestic manufacturing and the broader distribution market. Domestic manufacturing revenue declined at -1.5% CAGR between 2020 and 2025, driven by the inability of Canadian plants to compete with lower-cost imports and the rationalization of production facilities by multinational owners.xviii However, IBISWorld projects that the domestic manufacturing segment is expected to grow over the next five years (2025-2030), driven by nearshoring trends, tariff protection effects, and increased demand from mining and renewable energy sectors.

Import volumes have shown resilience despite trade disruptions. Canada's mining sector, concentrated in Northern Ontario, Quebec, and Western Canada, is a significant consumer of heavy-duty bearings - particularly spherical roller bearings, tapered roller bearings, and large-bore cylindrical roller bearings used in crushers, grinding mills, conveyors, and vibrating screens. Much of Canada's industrial equipment base - including manufacturing machinery, HVAC systems, building infrastructure, and agricultural equipment - is aging and requires increasing levels of maintenance, including bearing replacement. Electric vehicles use fewer but more specialized bearings than internal combustion vehicles, while wind turbines require large-diameter main shaft bearings, pitch bearings, and yaw bearings. As a secondary benefit, importing directly into Canada under MFN duties of free to 6.5% avoids the embedded US Section 301 tariffs of 7.5-25% that inflate costs for Chinese-origin bearings transiting through American intermediaries.

The large incumbent Canadian distributors - BDI/Canadian Bearings, Motion Canada, Wajax - have not replicated this direct-import model because their operations are structured as branch extensions of US parent companies with continental supply chains, and their authorized distributor agreements with OEM manufacturers (SKF, NTN, Timken) contractually bind them to purchasing through established channels. Importing generic Asian-origin bearings alongside premium branded products would risk their authorized dealer status - the single strongest structural barrier, rooted in incentive incompatibility rather than capability.

The direct-import opportunity is, however, open to any independent operator with Asian sourcing capability - including Chinese manufacturers or trading companies that may possess deeper supplier relationships and greater scale than 4S International. The reason this gap has persisted is not that no one possesses the sourcing expertise, but that (a) large incumbents face structural barriers to adopting the model, (b) Chinese manufacturers have focused on B2B production rather than Canadian last-mile distribution (as discussed in Section 5.2), and (c) the Canadian SME bearing segment, while underserved, is not large enough to attract major new entrants who would need to build local warehousing, logistics, customs compliance, and customer service infrastructure from scratch. 4S International's business case does not depend on being the only company capable of direct Asian sourcing; it depends on executing a viable business model in a market where the opportunity currently exists and where the Company's founder has documented, operational sourcing capability.

Second, the Company's multi-country sourcing portfolio provides supply chain redundancy that is unavailable to distributors authorized for a single manufacturer's product line. If trade conditions, quality issues, or production disruptions affect any single source country, the Company can shift procurement to alternative suppliers without interrupting customer service.

Third, the Company's digital-first distribution model - built around a transactional e-commerce platform with real-time inventory visibility and cross-reference search - addresses the documented e-commerce gap in the Canadian bearing market. Among the major distributors, online purchasing is typically limited to gated portals requiring pre-established accounts; among SME distributors, only Bearings Canada operates a meaningful online store.

Fourth, the Company's purpose-built focus on the SME customer segment - with minimum order quantities as low as a single case, dedicated account management, and flexible payment terms - addresses the service gap created by the consolidation of the distribution tier around enterprise-focused, multi-branch operators.
